Background

Down syndrome

Formerly Mongolism Down’s syndrome Down’s Trisomy 21 Trisomy G

One in 800-1000
children per year are
born with the disorder

Documented by British doctor
John Langdon Down
in a medical journal
from 1866

[Their] hair is…of a brownish colour, straight and scanty. Their face is flat and broad. The cheeks are roundish. The eyes are obliquely placed…

Appearance

Smaller body

Face size is in the lowest
3%, though visually normal
relative to the smaller body

Smaller face

Facial features, including
ears, nose, and mouth,
shrink alongside.

Smaller mouth

Randomly ordered
and unusually shaped
teeth

Smaller mouth

A relatively large
tongue may protrude
uncontrollably

Problems

 Muscular Digestive Respiratory Circulatory Sensory Neurological

Low muscle tone

Muscles throughout the
body are more relaxed
and harder to control

Congenital digestive defects

10% of infants

Digestive defects that
are present in people with
Down syndrome from birth

Indigestion

There may be narrowing
or blockage of the
small intestine

Choking

An opening between the
trachea and esophagus lets
food to go into the windpipe

Celiac disease

A disease where gluten
digestion causes damage
to the small intestine

Gastroesophageal reflux

A disorder where acidic
foods in the stomach back
up into the esophagus

Nasal blockage

A smaller nose and
nasal passage can be
easily congested

Lung infections

Pneumonia and bronchitis
inflame the lungs and other
parts of the respiratory tract

Sleep apnea

A temporary and
involuntary stoppage of
breathing during sleep

Sleep apnea

Caused by a relatively
larger tongue or cell mass
at the back of the throat

AVSD

30-40% of cases

A opening is created
between the two atriums
of the human heart

AVSD

30-40% of cases

Oxygenated and
deoxygenated blood can
accidentally mix

AVSD

30-40% of cases

The heart has to grow
larger and work harder,
shortening the lifespan

Leukemia

10-20x higher risk

A cancer in which stem
cells mutate and displace
regular white blood cells

Hearing loss

Mild to severe in 50% of cases

Infants with the syndrome
often have a blocked
ear drainage tube

Hearing loss

Mild to severe 50% of cases

Fluid collects in the
middle ear, preventing
sound from passing through

Vision problems

70% of cases

Higher risks of having
eye defects ranging from
blurriness to blindness

Astigmatism

20% of cases

An irregular eye shape
prevents light rays
from focusing

Strabismus

Crossed eyes

Eye muscle imbalance can
cause the brain to ignore
one eye, turning it blind

Mental retardation

highly varying

Most have normal or
near-normal IQs, ranging
from 40 to 70

Mental retardation

highly varying

Mainly affects areas
related to communication,
such as speech

Alzeimer’s

Increased risks

Characteristics of the
memory loss disease develop
at a much younger age

Alzeimer’s

Increased risks

A protein linked to
early-onset Alzeimer’s is
triplicated in Down’s

Experience

 Early 20th century Mid 20th century Present

Low life expectancy

In 1910, those with Down
syndrome on average lived
to be 9 years old

Discrimination

The pejorative
Mongolian idiots
was widely used

Forced sterilization

Supported by the Supreme
Court, neurologically ill people
were forced to be sterilized

Holocaust

A majority of disabled people
targeted during the Holocaust
have Down syndrome

Overlooked by doctors

Infants were denied
heart surgeries and
sometimes even food

Improving life expectancy

In the 1940s, the average
Down syndrome case lived
to 20 years old

Present

80% of adults with
Down syndrome now live
to be 55 or older

Present

Americans with Disabilities
Act (ADA) bans discrimination
of people with disabilities

Causes

 Chromosomal level Family level Gene level

Nondisjunction

95% of cases

Chromosomes of the 21st
pair fail to separate
during meiosis

Nondisjunction

95% of cases

Resulting in a gamete
with one extra
chromosome 21

Nondisjunction

95% of cases

Most nondisjunction
cases come from the
maternal side

Nondisjunction

95% of cases

Risk increases as a mother
ages and her chromosomes
become stickier

Translocation

4% of cases

After nondisjunction, the
extra chromosome breaks off
and joins another pair

Mosaicism

1% of cases

Instead of meiosis,
nondisjunction occurs
during the mitosis

Mosaicism

1% of cases

The offspring has a mix
of cells with trisomy 21
and cells without

Nondisjunction carrier

1% likelihood of recurrence

If one child has Down’s
from nondisjunction, the next
is more likely to it too

Nondisjunction carrier

1% likelihood of recurrence

For mothers under 30,
the risk is ten times
more than normal

Translocation carrier

2-100% likelihood of recurrence

The parent may have
a translocation in
their karyotype

Translocation carrier

2-100% likelihood of recurrence

This could be detected, but
recurrence still varies widely
by sex and other factors

One parent has Down’s

50% of offspring affected

The parent have a 50%
likelihood of producing
trisomy 21 gametes

Gene mapping

A number of locations on
chromosome 21 are linked
to Down syndrome features

Discovery of DYRK

A gene producing the
protein DYRK was linked to
abnormal brain development

Detection

 Prenatal screening Prenatal diagnostic Post-birth symptoms

Prenatal screening

3-4 screenings are moderately
accurate at detecting the
syndrome in fetuses

Alpha fetoprotein (AFP)

15-20 weeks into pregnancy

A protein produced in the liver
of the fetus and circulating
in the mother’s blood

Alpha fetoprotein (AFP)

15-20 weeks into pregnancy

High levels suggest birth
defects, while low levels
imply Down syndrome

Nuchal translucency scan

An ultrasound of the
fetus’s neck which detects
chromosome abnormalities

Prenatal diagnostic

Done if screening tests show
positives, they produce
more definitive results

Chorionic villus sampling

9-12 weeks into pregnancy

Samples the womb
cells that nourish
the fetus

Amniocentesis

14-18 weeks into pregnancy

Genetic analysis on shed
skin cells of the fetus
in the amniotic fluids

Low muscle tone

Immediately after birth

Muscles are weaker than
normal infants, only
contracting partially

Treatment

 Physical defects Mental defects

Medication

Respiratory infections

Advancements in antibiotics
can treat many infections
common with the syndrome

Physical therapy

Low muscle tone

Physical therapy can
sometimes increase
muscle strength

Surgery

Heart & digestive defects

Open-heart and intestinal
surgeries can often restore
conditions to normal

Speech therapy

Therapists can help
children with Down syndrome
learn speech alongside peers

Medication

New drugs undergoing
testing may solve the memory
and learning problems.

...recent advances in neuroscience and genetics are starting to raise the prospect of pharmaceutical interventions. Several clinical trials of drugs to improve memory and learning in adults with Down syndrome are now under way.
